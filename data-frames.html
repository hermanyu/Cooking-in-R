Chapter 4 Data Frames

A data frame is a table or spreadsheet. Abstractly, a data frame can be thought of as a matrix with named rows and columns. To create a data frame, we supply the columns of the table

# create a data frame of car sales for 6 months
car_sales_df <- data.frame(
  row.names = c("january", "february", "march", "april", "may", "june"), # set row index
  compact = c(26, 35, 18, 22, 28, 40),  # column of compact car sales by month
  fullsize = c(22, 26, 24, 25, 21, 32), # column of fullsize car sales by month
  suv = c(12, 20, 17, 12, 20, 26),      # column of SUV care sales by month
  pickup = c(8, 6, 6, 12, 11, 18),      # column of pickup truck car sales by month
  sports_coupe = c(6, 8, 4, 1, 8, 3)    # column of sports coupe car sales by month
)

car_sales_df
##          compact fullsize suv pickup sports_coupe
## january       26       22  12      8            6
## february      35       26  20      6            8
## march         18       24  17      6            4
## april         22       25  12     12            1
## may           28       21  20     11            8
## june          40       32  26     18            3


4.1 Summary and Structure

Data frames are meant to store and organize data sets for analysis and manipulation. Therefore, the very first functions we should consider are the summary() and str() functions, which quickly summarizes the data and metadata of the data frame.

We can summarize the data frame by calling the summary() function. This will return the standard 5 number summary (min, 1Q, median, 3Q, max) and the mean, for each column.

summary(car_sales_df)
##     compact         fullsize          suv            pickup       sports_coupe 
##  Min.   :18.00   Min.   :21.00   Min.   :12.00   Min.   : 6.00   Min.   :1.00  
##  1st Qu.:23.00   1st Qu.:22.50   1st Qu.:13.25   1st Qu.: 6.50   1st Qu.:3.25  
##  Median :27.00   Median :24.50   Median :18.50   Median : 9.50   Median :5.00  
##  Mean   :28.17   Mean   :25.00   Mean   :17.83   Mean   :10.17   Mean   :5.00  
##  3rd Qu.:33.25   3rd Qu.:25.75   3rd Qu.:20.00   3rd Qu.:11.75   3rd Qu.:7.50  
##  Max.   :40.00   Max.   :32.00   Max.   :26.00   Max.   :18.00   Max.   :8.00


We can also obtain the “structure” of the data frame, which returns the column names, the typing, and the values.

str(car_sales_df)
## 'data.frame':    6 obs. of  5 variables:
##  $ compact     : num  26 35 18 22 28 40
##  $ fullsize    : num  22 26 24 25 21 32
##  $ suv         : num  12 20 17 12 20 26
##  $ pickup      : num  8 6 6 12 11 18
##  $ sports_coupe: num  6 8 4 1 8 3


We can also quickly take a peak at our data frame by calling the head() function, which returns the first few rows of the data frame

# get head of data frame, up to 3rd row
head(car_sales_df, 3)
##          compact fullsize suv pickup sports_coupe
## january       26       22  12      8            6
## february      35       26  20      6            8
## march         18       24  17      6            4


If there is a head(), then it makes sense to have a tail() function too!

# get tail of data frame, up to 3rd from last row
tail(car_sales_df, 3)
##       compact fullsize suv pickup sports_coupe
## april      22       25  12     12            1
## may        28       21  20     11            8
## june       40       32  26     18            3


And finally, we can quickly see the shape of the data frame using the dim() function.

# get the dimensions of the data frame.
dim(car_sales_df)
## [1] 6 5


We see that the data frame is of shape \(6 \times 5\); 6 rows (observations) with 5 columns (features).


4.2 Extracting and Subsetting

To extract values from the data frame, we use the bracket syntax dataframe[i] to access the \(i\)-th column of the data frame. However, because data frames have named columns and rows, we can actually access then in multiple different ways (because columns can be identified in multiple different ways).

# get the "compact" column of the car_sales_df by specifying its index
car_sales_df[1]
##          compact
## january       26
## february      35
## march         18
## april         22
## may           28
## june          40
# get the "compact" column of car_sales_df by specifying its name "month"
car_sales_df["compact"]
##          compact
## january       26
## february      35
## march         18
## april         22
## may           28
## june          40


Notice that this returns a data.frame object with a single column. However, since columns of a data frame are just vectors, we might want to return the column as a vector. This can be done by using double brackes [[...]]

# return the "compact" column as a vector
car_sales_df[[1]]
## [1] 26 35 18 22 28 40
# return the "compact" column as a vector
car_sales_df[["compact"]]
## [1] 26 35 18 22 28 40
# return the "compact" column as a vector
car_sales_df$compact
## [1] 26 35 18 22 28 40


Note: Look! This is our first encounter with a signature R syntax, the dollar sign $ which is used to reference columns.

More important note: Notice that car_sales_df[["month"]] returns a vector and not a data frame. This is in polar opposition to Pandas in Python, where the syntax car_sales_df[["month"]] would return a data frame.

There are 2 ways to think about data frames.

  1. A data frame is a list of vectors (where vector = column).
  2. A data frame is a matrix with named rows and columns.

Because a data frame can be thought of in these 2 ways, the syntax for both vectors and matrices will generalize effortlessly to data frames.

To reference/subset elements of a vector, we pass indices. Since a data frame is just

# get 1st, 2nd, and 4th columns
car_sales_df[c(1,2,4)]
##          compact fullsize pickup
## january       26       22      8
## february      35       26      6
## march         18       24      6
## april         22       25     12
## may           28       21     11
## june          40       32     18

 

Since data frames are just matrices with named columns, we can certainly pass a vector of column names too!

# return columns by name
car_sales_df[c("compact", "fullsize", "pickup")]
##          compact fullsize pickup
## january       26       22      8
## february      35       26      6
## march         18       24      6
## april         22       25     12
## may           28       21     11
## june          40       32     18


And like matrices, we can grab entire slices along columns

# return columns 2 to 5
car_sales_df[2:5]
##          fullsize suv pickup sports_coupe
## january        22  12      8            6
## february       26  20      6            8
## march          24  17      6            4
## april          25  12     12            1
## may            21  20     11            8
## june           32  26     18            3


Data frames can also be thought of as matrices with named rows and columns, thus matrix indexing for rows and columns will work perfectly well for data frames.

# return the first row of car_sales_df
car_sales_df[1,]
##         compact fullsize suv pickup sports_coupe
## january      26       22  12      8            6


But since rows are named, we can also reference rows by their names (aka index aka key):

car_sales_df["january",]
##         compact fullsize suv pickup sports_coupe
## january      26       22  12      8            6


And we can grab subsets and slices like usual:

# grab rows 1, 2, and 5
car_sales_df[c(1,2,5),]
##          compact fullsize suv pickup sports_coupe
## january       26       22  12      8            6
## february      35       26  20      6            8
## may           28       21  20     11            8
# grab a subset of rows by name
car_sales_df[c("january", "february", "may"),]
##          compact fullsize suv pickup sports_coupe
## january       26       22  12      8            6
## february      35       26  20      6            8
## may           28       21  20     11            8
# grab a slice of rows 2 to 5
car_sales_df[2:5,]
##          compact fullsize suv pickup sports_coupe
## february      35       26  20      6            8
## march         18       24  17      6            4
## april         22       25  12     12            1
## may           28       21  20     11            8


And we can reference individual entries like in matrices too:

# grab entry at row 1, column 1
car_sales_df[1,1]
## [1] 26
# grab entry at row "january", column "compact"
car_sales_df["january", "compact"]
## [1] 26
# grab a sub-data frame of february to may for compact, fullsize, and sports coupes
car_sales_df[2:5, c("compact", "fullsize", "sports_coupe")]
##          compact fullsize sports_coupe
## february      35       26            8
## march         18       24            4
## april         22       25            1
## may           28       21            8


4.3 Editing, Adding and Removing Columns

Being able to reference subsets of the data frame also allows us to change the stored values. For example, let’s say we were told that that the sports_coupe column in car_sales_df was actually missing the sales from some dealership. The actual values for the sports_coupe column is given by:

# actual sports coupe sales numbers
actual_sports_coupe <- c(14, 14, 10, 3, 12, 7)

actual_sports_coupe
## [1] 14 14 10  3 12  7


What we want to do is overwrite sports_coupe column with car_sales_df. To do this, we need to reference the column and assign it the correct vector of values:

# overwrite sports_coupe column with actual_sports_coupe vector
car_sales_df["sports_coupe"] <- actual_sports_coupe

car_sales_df
##          compact fullsize suv pickup sports_coupe
## january       26       22  12      8           14
## february      35       26  20      6           14
## march         18       24  17      6           10
## april         22       25  12     12            3
## may           28       21  20     11           12
## june          40       32  26     18            7


Of course, we had multiple ways of referencing columns, so there are multiple ways to carry out this overwrite procedure:

# define a new vector of sports coupe vectors
even_more_sports_coupe <- c(20, 20, 12, 20, 20, 20)

# overwrite sports_coupe column with even_more_sports_coupe vector
car_sales_df$sports_coupe <- even_more_sports_coupe

car_sales_df
##          compact fullsize suv pickup sports_coupe
## january       26       22  12      8           20
## february      35       26  20      6           20
## march         18       24  17      6           12
## april         22       25  12     12           20
## may           28       21  20     11           20
## june          40       32  26     18           20


Similarly, we can overwrite row data as well.

# define a new vector for january sales
mega_jan <- c(50, 50, 30, 20, 20)

# overwrite january row with mega_jan vector
car_sales_df["january",] <- mega_jan

car_sales_df
##          compact fullsize suv pickup sports_coupe
## january       50       50  30     20           20
## february      35       26  20      6           20
## march         18       24  17      6           12
## april         22       25  12     12           20
## may           28       21  20     11           20
## june          40       32  26     18           20
